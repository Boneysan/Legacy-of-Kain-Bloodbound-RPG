# Arc II: Ascension Choice Consequence Guide

Use this document beside 04_Choice-Guide.md when you want each fortress or breach decision to change the next scene, the next faction move, or the next strategic problem.

## Choose a public position

### Encounter Uses

- Shelter under a claimant supports courtly protection scenes, formal guard duty, and faction fights where the party are visibly attached to one banner.
- Independent or outlaw positioning supports checkpoint pressure, barracks refusal, and survival encounters where no one offers safe ground.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Taking shelter buys immediate access and food, but the fortress reads every action afterward as loyalty.
- Refusing formal backing makes the party freer in motion, but every door opens slower and every rumor turns sharper.

### Faction Pressure

- Backing a claimant strengthens that bloc and pushes its rivals toward grievance, challenge, or covert retaliation.
- Declaring independence irritates factions that equate neutrality with hidden ambition, especially in a fortress built on command logic.

### Later Payoffs

- A claimant who sheltered the party later expects repayment in blood, public endorsement, or fragment access.
- Operating outside protection can become a legend of integrity or a pretext for everyone to deny the party shelter when the north worsens.

## Choose the method of intervention

### Encounter Uses

- Formal challenge supports duels, oath-bound combat, and public scenes where the whole fortress watches legitimacy being made.
- Infiltration or exposure supports stealth, archives, sabotage recovery, and proof-driven encounters under a rising threat of discovery.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Public challenge clarifies who won the room, even if it solves little beneath the spectacle.
- Covert action or investigation reveals deeper rot, but if exposed it immediately stains the party's claim to honorable judgment.

### Faction Pressure

- Martial clans and traditionalists respect visible challenge more than hidden manipulation.
- The Pale Accord and Sarafan legalists value proof, but will still weaponize how that proof was obtained.

### Later Payoffs

- A public challenge creates blood-debts, insult memory, and surviving witnesses who remember exactly who stood where.
- Covert success can later be reframed as fraud, letting defeated claimants deny the verdict and reignite the dispute.

## Decide whether legitimacy is granted, exposed, or refused

### Encounter Uses

- Granting legitimacy supports coronation defense, crackdown scenes, or targeted violence from those who refuse the settlement.
- Exposing or refusing supports riot containment, evacuation, or scramble encounters as command structures fracture in real time.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Granting a ruler gives the fortress a working chain of command before the party march north.
- Exposing the true claim or refusing legitimacy makes the room morally clearer and politically more dangerous at once.

### Faction Pressure

- Clans move toward cooperation if their view of rule is validated, and toward open feud if it is publicly humiliated.
- Sarafan legalists prefer a legible settlement, while hardliners benefit from bloodshed proving vampire rule cannot govern itself.

### Later Payoffs

- A recognized ruler later sends troops, tribute, or hunters depending on whether the party preserved that ruler's dignity.
- Refused legitimacy feeds the Clan War clock, making the fortress remain a live wound instead of background scenery.

## Decide the Iron Echo's status

### Encounter Uses

- Open use supports fragment rite scenes, challenge-interruption battles, and moments where command itself becomes the weapon.
- Sealing, hiding, or shared stewardship supports vault defense, convoy protection, or tense councils vulnerable to assassination.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Open use lets the party end the immediate crisis decisively, but everyone present learns the fragment can settle power by force of memory.
- Sealing or shared stewardship postpones finality, so the fortress leaves the session quieter but more brittle.

### Faction Pressure

- Open use empowers whichever bloc can claim the Echo's sanction and alarms Balance-minded observers immediately.
- Hidden or stewarded status calms some claimants, but guarantees later disputes over who really controls succession.

### Later Payoffs

- If the Echo was used openly, later claimants cite that precedent whenever fragment logic can solve a political deadlock.
- If it was sealed or stewarded, later raids, theft attempts, and legal claims cluster around whoever holds the key.

## Decide who comes north, who stays behind, and what cannot be carried further

### Encounter Uses

- Bringing envoys, prisoners, or marked survivors supports escort fights, divided-loyalty travel, and scenes where the northern front starts already crowded.
- Leaving unstable burdens behind supports rear-guard departures, mutiny risk, or later strike scenes when what was left behind explodes politically.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- What travels north decides whether the expedition feels like a military task, a refugee burden, or a moving tribunal.
- What is left behind immediately changes who controls the story inside Dumah's Fortress after the party depart.

### Faction Pressure

- Bringing claim-marked survivors north imports fortress politics into Hylden territory and keeps clan pressure on the party's heels.
- Leaving key people behind gives local rulers, Sarafan observers, or rivals the chance to reshape their testimony without the party present.

### Later Payoffs

- A prisoner or envoy brought north can later become leverage in a breach negotiation or a liability during a panic.
- A survivor left behind can later reappear as a claimant's mouthpiece, a martyr, or someone broken by the very order the party left in place.

## Use enemy tools, destroy them, or attempt a third path

### Encounter Uses

- Using Hylden tools supports device-activation encounters, possession defense, and scenes where the machinery works but the cost is visible.
- Destroying the tools or forcing a third path supports demolition runs, unstable lattice repair, or improvised sealing under incomplete knowledge.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Using the tools gives the party a practical answer now, but everyone present feels the metaphysical contamination of the choice.
- Destroying them or pursuing a third path preserves principle, but it can make the immediate crisis bloodier and less certain.

### Faction Pressure

- Hylden negotiators press hardest when necessity starts sounding persuasive, while the Accord and Wardens lean toward alternatives that do not normalize enemy logic.
- The Crimson Fracture gains rhetoric whenever the party prove the old boundaries only hold through borrowed monstrosity or desperate improvisation.

### Later Payoffs

- A successful Hylden-derived solution later stains trust, making allies ask whether the party are still resisting or merely managing a better prison.
- A third-path prototype or a destroyed tool cache becomes a later target for scholars, saboteurs, or survivors desperate for one more chance.

## Seal, redirect, weaponize, or survive failure

### Encounter Uses

- Sealing supports hold-the-line encounters around the lattice, while redirecting supports evacuation or defense of whichever place now inherits the danger.
- Weaponizing or surviving failure supports catastrophic battlefield scenes, extraction under collapse, and enemy waves empowered by instability.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Sealing makes the party look like saviors, but only if the cost is survivable and the seal feels real.
- Redirecting, weaponizing, or failing immediately reframes the party as pragmatists, opportunists, or the authors of a worse scar.

### Faction Pressure

- Legalists, exhausted settlements, and practical allies favor a seal, even if it is ugly.
- Hylden voices, Fracture agitators, and hostile rivals all gain leverage if the breach becomes a weapon, a relocation, or a public disaster.

### Later Payoffs

- The scar left by the breach can return through refugees, unstable terrain, possession rumors, or Ash-Glass converts who call the event revelation.
- Any contradiction born here should later reappear as the concrete reason Arc III must go to the Chronoplast.
