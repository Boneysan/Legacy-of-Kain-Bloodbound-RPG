# Arc IV: Convergence Choice Consequence Guide

Use this document beside 05_Choice-Guide.md when you want the endgame choice structure to drive encounter framing, coalition collapse, and the final shape of the aftermath.

## Decide who is permitted near the party, the Pillars, and the assembled Heart

### Encounter Uses

- A narrow coalition supports elite infiltration, shortage-driven defense, and scenes where the party must do more with fewer hands.
- A broad coalition or total exclusion supports multi-front standoffs, sabotage, and immediate violence from any bloc told it has no place near the verdict.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Narrow access gives the final march ideological clarity, but every absent specialist, army, or witness is felt at once.
- Broad access buys manpower and legitimacy, but fills every scene with conflicting agendas before the Hall is even reached.

### Faction Pressure

- Included factions behave like stakeholders and demand to be heard when sacrifice begins.
- Excluded factions move quickly toward open sabotage, last offers, or irreversible betrayal because they know they are being written out of history.

### Later Payoffs

- A rejected bloc can later become the spoiler that nearly breaks final execution or poisons the aftermath.
- A broad coalition preserves more witnesses to the ending, but also more survivors ready to contest what it meant.

## Choose sacrifice, compromise, or refusal on the road to the Hall

### Encounter Uses

- Sacrifice supports rear-guard, ordeal, or passage-earned-through-loss encounters that prove intent in blood rather than argument.
- Compromise or refusal supports negotiation under arms, forced alternate routes, or access-denial scenes where time itself becomes the enemy.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Sacrifice gets the party through with moral weight, but it visibly empties their ranks, standing, or innocence.
- Compromise preserves numbers and access, while refusal preserves integrity and costs momentum; both choices make the next scene politically charged.

### Faction Pressure

- Wardens and hard judges respect real cost more than clever rhetoric.
- Compromise validates pragmatic blocs, while refusal energizes purists and alienates any ally who needed the Hall opened now.

### Later Payoffs

- A sacrificial passage creates martyrs, debts, and survivors who can later testify that the ending was paid for honestly.
- A compromise or refusal can later be cited as proof that the party bought access dirty or let principle endanger the world.

## Commit to an endgame direction at the Black Fulcrum

### Encounter Uses

- Breaking the Wheel or stabilizing the breach supports split-field encounters where one front protects the Fulcrum and another contains the immediate metaphysical consequence.
- Imposing order or forcing merge or fracture supports suppression battles, mass panic, and scenes where the battlefield itself starts choosing predators and victims.
- A burdened compromise supports multi-objective play: hold the chamber, preserve key seals, and keep enough witnesses alive that the verdict remains morally legible.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Anti-Wheel commitment makes freedom the declared priority, even if the sky answers with danger at once.
- Breach-first commitment makes survival and material stability the declared priority, even if it feels like leaving a deeper tyranny standing.
- Order, fracture, or compromise immediately reveal which allies were waiting for stewardship, domination, or collapse all along.

### Faction Pressure

- Breaking the Wheel hardens legal-order blocs, Wheel loyalists, and anyone terrified of uncontrolled aftermath.
- Stabilizing the breach angers Kainites, anti-Wheel radicals, and every survivor who reads delay as surrender.
- Imposed order creates one ascendant bloc and many bitter enemies, while merge logic empowers Fracture and catastrophe-faith actors immediately.

### Later Payoffs

- A wounded Wheel leaves later soul-order problems, unrest among the dead, and a world freer but less governed.
- A stabilized breach leaves stronger institutions and a surviving anti-Wheel grievance that does not believe the real work was done.
- Imposed order creates the next age's insurgency, while merge or compromise leaves behind pilgrims, predators, or custodians bound to ongoing instability.

## Decide what to protect when every front is collapsing

### Encounter Uses

- Prioritizing chamber control supports close defense, ritual interruption, and brutally concentrated fights over the core mechanism of the ending.
- Prioritizing battlefield control or witness extraction supports command fights, rescue lanes, and scenes where history depends on who survives to name it.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Chamber priority keeps the chosen answer executable, but can make victory feel purchased through abandonment.
- Battlefield or survivor priority preserves people and legitimacy, but risks losing the one moment the Fulcrum can still be shaped.

### Faction Pressure

- Allies stranded by the party's priority choice react immediately, especially if they bled for inclusion and then were left to hold a doomed line.
- Balance-minded observers care deeply about whether the party preserved a verdict, a battlefield, or the living meaning of the campaign.

### Later Payoffs

- Survivors saved here become the witnesses, rulers, accusers, or martyrs who define the ending afterward.
- A front abandoned here becomes the grievance every losing faction carries into the next age.

## Carry the final choice through to completion

### Encounter Uses

- If the party accept visible cost, frame the last encounter as a sacrificial completion under direct assault, with no illusion that victory is clean.
- If they try to conceal the price, frame the finish around interruption, witness survival, or a final push by those who know false balance when they see it.

### Immediate Narrative Changes

- Visible cost makes the ending legible within scenes: the Pillars, the wound, and the coalition all answer the choice at once.
- Concealed cost may keep order for a breath, but the final scene should already feel unstable, partial, or morally counterfeit.

### Faction Pressure

- Wardens, practical survivors, and truth-minded allies accept harsh endings more readily when the price is openly borne.
- Legalists, rivals, and surviving witnesses become far more dangerous if they believe the party hid what the ending really demanded.

### Later Payoffs

- An openly paid ending produces legends, burdens, and a verdict history can defend even if it remains tragic.
- A concealed ending produces tribunals, heresies, and a later collapse of legitimacy when the hidden cost inevitably surfaces.
