# Arc I: Awakening Choice Guide

Use this document to track the arc's major narrative decisions. This guide focuses on choices that materially change faction standing, relic custody, trust, or the public story leaving Avernus.

## Session 1: Roadside Priorities

### Defend people, cargo, or information

- Defend people: preserves lives and goodwill, but risks losing the reliquary, courier leverage, or the initiative.
- Defend cargo: protects the immediate mission, but may cost witnesses, refugees, or the party's moral standing.
- Defend information: prioritizes clues, suspects, and proof, but can leave both civilians and cargo exposed.
- Carry forward: determines who trusts the party after the ambush, what survives the road to Avernus, and whether Session 2 opens from rescue, loss, or suspicion.

## Session 3: The Descent Below Avernus

### Choose speed, secrecy, or safety

- Descend fast: reach the Blood-Well sooner, but invite ambush, confusion, and unfinished consequences behind the party.
- Proceed carefully: reduce immediate risk, but give the cult time to prepare, relocate, or complete part of its rite.
- Detour to save captives, witnesses, or stolen cargo: preserves people and evidence, but costs time and surprise.
- Carry forward: shapes who reaches the chamber alive, how prepared the cult is, and whether the party arrive as hunters, rescuers, or late witnesses.

## Session 4: First Contact At The Blood-Well

### Press for truth now or limit contact

- Push for deeper truth immediately: gains revelation, names, and blood-memory insight, but increases Corruption, exposure, and personal fallout.
- Limit contact and prioritize survival: preserves the party's position and reduces immediate harm, but leaves key questions unresolved and gives rivals interpretive space.
- Carry forward: determines how much the party learns before the chamber crisis escalates and who pays the first personal price for contact with the Sanguine Knot.

## Session 5: Fate Of The Sanguine Knot

### Take, seal, conceal, or temporarily leave the fragment

- Take it: grants direct leverage and future access, but makes the party the obvious center of every subsequent pursuit.
- Seal it: reduces immediate misuse, but may leave the fragment accessible to whoever controls the site later.
- Conceal it: denies open ownership, but creates long-term secrecy problems and invites betrayal.
- Temporarily leave it: buys time under pressure, but risks surrendering initiative to the Accord, the cult, or the Sarafan.
- Carry forward: decides the fragment's short-term custody and locks in who starts Arc II with leverage, grievance, and a reason to pursue.

### Decide who controls relic access and chamber truth

- Keep control inside the party: maximizes independence, but increases internal burden and faction hostility.
- Share controlled access with an ally: gains expertise or cover, but creates dependency and future claims.
- Let a faction define the chamber narrative: may stabilize the exit, but gives others the power to rewrite what happened below Avernus.
- Carry forward: influences whether the party leave with authority, deniability, or a dangerous half-truth.

## Session 6: The Story That Leaves Avernus

### Decide who travels with the party

- Bring key survivors or experts: gains witnesses, guides, and leverage, but adds vulnerability and political baggage.
- Leave people behind under protection or watch: reduces travel pressure, but risks abandonment, capture, or conflicting testimony.
- Carry forward: shapes Arc II's cast, obligations, and who can speak credibly about the Blood-Well.

### Choose what version of the truth enters the world

- Tell the truth broadly: creates urgency and moral clarity, but accelerates purges, panic, and opportunism.
- Share only with selected allies: preserves leverage and control, but deepens mistrust and future accusations of manipulation.
- Bury or blur the truth: limits immediate fallout, but cedes narrative ground to factions willing to lie faster.
- Carry forward: determines whether Arc II begins under scandal, secrecy, or contested rumor.
