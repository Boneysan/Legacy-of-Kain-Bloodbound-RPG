## Vampires

Once stewards of balance, now immortal predators fractured by Kain's defiance, the vampire broods echo their progenitors' power and flaws. Their houses grew territorial and bestial as the Pillars fell and time twisted. Every clan is a theology of hunger—part empire, part curse.

Sublineages and notes — The Six Clans:

### Culture and Customs

Vampire society is stratified by blood and age. Elders rule through dominance, memory, and the sheer weight of centuries. Fledglings serve, learn, and scheme. The middle ranks—the "Blooded"—form the backbone of clan politics: enforcers, diplomats, artisans, and spies.

- **Naming:** Vampires take a *sire-name* upon turning (granted by their maker) and a *blood-name* earned through deed or reputation. Using a vampire's mortal name is a profound insult—it implies they are still human. Example: *Varek Ironthirst* (sire-name: Varek, blood-name: Ironthirst).
- **Art:** Each clan expresses differently. Turelim carve monumental stone. Zephonim weave tapestries of silk and shadow. Dumahim sculpt silence—empty rooms arranged with mathematical precision. Razielim left behind spectral etchings visible only in the Spectral Realm. Melchiahim create grotesque flesh-art. Rahabim compose underwater echo-songs.
- **Blood Rites:** All clans observe the *Crimson Toast*—a shared feeding from a single vessel to seal alliances, truces, or pacts. Breaking a Crimson Toast is the gravest vampire sin, punishable by exile or immurement.
- **The Long Sleep:** Vampires who tire of immortality or lose purpose enter a voluntary torpor called the Long Sleep, interring themselves in clan crypts. Waking a Long Sleeper without invitation is sacrilege. Some have slept for centuries and remember a Nosgoth that no longer exists.

### Thralls and Human Servants

No vampire society functions without mortal hands. Beneath the broods and the blood-politics exists an entire human underclass—servants, worshippers, and cattle—whose labor, loyalty, and lives sustain the immortal courts.

- **Blood-Bound Thralls:** Mortals who have consumed vampire blood and become chemically dependent on it. The blood grants minor physical enhancements (resistance to disease, slowed aging, heightened senses) but creates an unbreakable psychological compulsion to serve the vampire who fed them. Thralls are fiercely loyal, not because they choose to be, but because withdrawal from the blood is agonizing. Most thralls cannot distinguish love from addiction. Many don't try.
- **Willing Worshippers:** Humans who serve vampire masters voluntarily—out of faith, ambition, or the cold calculation that servitude to an immortal is safer than freedom in Nosgoth. Some worship vampires as gods; others see the arrangement as a transaction (protection for obedience). The Turelim and Dumahim maintain the most structured thrall-hierarchies, with human stewards managing daylight logistics, tribute collection, and mortal-facing diplomacy.
- **Cattle:** The darkest stratum. Humans kept purely as a blood supply—sometimes in relative comfort (fed, housed, and cared for like prized livestock), sometimes in conditions that would horrify even other vampires. Melchiahim settlements are feared because their "cattle" provide not just blood but *flesh* for the Mending. Rahabim keep few thralls—their aquatic territories make human habitation impractical—but coastal fishing villages sometimes pay a "tide-tax" in blood.
- **Thrall Culture:** Thralls develop their own customs within vampire courts. Senior thralls hold status and influence, acting as intermediaries between their masters and the mortal world. Some thrall families have served the same brood for generations, their children born into service. A freed thrall (rare) is an outcast in both worlds—too tainted for human society, too mortal for vampire courts.
- **In Play:** Thralls provide opportunities for social roleplaying, moral complexity, and low-level intrigue. A party might need to navigate a vampire court through its thrall-steward, rescue a blood-bound servant who doesn't want to be saved, or confront the ethics of a system where the livestock is grateful for its cage.

### Attitudes Toward the Wheel of Fate

Vampires exist outside the Wheel—their souls do not cycle through death and rebirth. This makes them invisible to the Elder God and deeply unsettling to those who serve it. Most vampires regard the Wheel with contempt ("a hamster wheel for mortals") or dread ("what happens to us when we finally die?"). A few—especially among the Razielim—believe Raziel's sacrifice created a crack in the Wheel through which vampire souls might one day pass. This heresy is called the *Razielite Exception* and is fiercely debated. *(See also: World Primer — Religions, Beliefs, and Philosophies for the parallel mortal faith: the* Razielite Martyrdom*, which venerates Raziel as a holy sacrifice among human communities.)*

### The Six Clans

#### Razielim
The lost clan. Most were annihilated by Kain after Raziel's execution. Survivors are incredibly rare anomalies, perhaps spared by stasis or resurrected through paradoxes. They are ghost-proud and often seek vengeance or the truth of their sire's fate.
- **Internal politics:** The few survivors are bound by the *Covenant of Embers*—a mutual oath to protect any Razielim found alive. This makes them intensely loyal to each other, but paranoid about infiltration. A false Razielim is worse than any enemy.
- **Coming-of-age:** The *Flight of Mourning*—a young Razielim glides from the highest point they can reach and does not use their wings until the last possible moment. The fall represents their clan's fall. The catch represents their refusal to die.
- **Daily life:** Razielim live in hidden enclaves, constantly moving. They communicate through *echo-marks*—spectral scratches on walls visible only to those with planar sensitivity. A city with echo-marks is safe; a city without them has been compromised.

#### Turelim
Warrior-kings with telekinetic force. Fortress builders and loyalist conquerors who served as Kain's heavy enforcement.
- **Internal politics:** Turelim operate on a strict military hierarchy called the *Stone Rank*. Promotion is earned through combat, construction, or strategic victory—never through politics. A Turelim who schemes is considered defective. This rigid meritocracy makes them predictable but devastatingly efficient.
- **Coming-of-age:** The *Siege Trial*—a fledgling must breach a fortified position designed by their elder using only their telekinetic gift and their wits. The trial is not about winning; it's about demonstrating the willingness to try. Those who refuse the trial are exiled.
- **Daily life:** Turelim territories are the most ordered in Nosgoth. Streets run straight. Walls are maintained. Blood-taxes are collected on schedule. This efficiency makes their territory attractive to mortal refugees willing to serve—and suffocating to anyone who values freedom.

#### Dumahim
Stoic titans—entombed, patient, relentless. They act as the shock troops and heavy infantry of the vampire legions.
- **Internal politics:** Dumahim do not debate. Decisions are made through the *Council of Silence*—clan members sit together until consensus forms naturally, sometimes for days. The first to speak loses the argument. This produces either profound unity or total paralysis.
- **Coming-of-age:** The *Entombment*—a young Dumahim is buried alive for a period determined by their elder (days to weeks). They emerge when they can break free through pure physical will. Those who panic are interred longer. Those who sleep through it are respected most.
- **Daily life:** Dumahim settlements are eerily quiet. Communication happens through gesture, expression, and shared presence. Visitors find the silence oppressive; Dumahim find noise vulgar. Their halls contain no music, no laughter—only the deep, rhythmic breathing of a clan that has learned to wait.

#### Zephonim
Parasitic mutations adapted to vertical ruins. Ambushers, secret-keepers, spider-like masters of ascent. Their long isolation in darkened, web-filled spires led them to perceive shadows not as an absence of light but as a tangible, weaveable substance—which is why they produce so many Shadowmancers.
- **Internal politics:** Zephonim organize through webs of obligation and secret—a system called the *Silk Accord*. Every transaction creates a thread of debt. Every secret learned adds a strand. The most connected Zephonim—those at the center of the most webs—rule not through strength but through leverage. If a powerful Zephonim dies, the secrets they held die with them, creating catastrophic power vacuums.
- **Coming-of-age:** The *Descent*—a young Zephonim is dropped into a lightless vertical ruin and must climb out carrying a specific object hidden within. The test is not of climbing ability (all Zephonim can climb) but of perception—finding the object in total darkness amid false trails and traps laid by elders.
- **Daily life:** Zephonim lairs are three-dimensional tangles of silk, stone, and shadow, built vertically rather than horizontally. Conversations happen across chasms. Meetings occur on ceilings. Outsiders experience vertigo; Zephonim experience open ground as existentially threatening.
- **Art:** They weave *whisper-tapestries*—silk constructions that vibrate at specific frequencies, encoding information in sound. A tapestry can be "read" by touching it and listening to its hum. Destroying a tapestry destroys the knowledge it contains.

#### Melchiahim
The flesh-weavers. Melchiah was the weakest of Kain's lieutenants, and his curse fell heaviest on his children—their bodies decay constantly, crumbling like wet clay. To survive, they must graft the flesh of others onto their own rotting frames, a process they call *The Mending*. This has made them Nosgoth's most visually horrifying vampires and its most resourceful survivors.
- **Internal politics:** Melchiahim hierarchy is based on *structural integrity*—the more successfully a vampire has Mended, the higher their status. A Melchiahim whose grafts are seamless and functional outranks one whose body is a patchwork of mismatched parts. The clan's greatest artisans are called *Flesh Architects*, and their work is considered both art and engineering.
- **Coming-of-age:** The *First Mending*—a young Melchiahim's body has begun to decay, and they must perform their first graft without assistance. The quality of this first Mending shapes their reputation for decades. Those who fail to Mend at all are allowed to decay completely—a mercy killing through inaction.
- **Daily life:** Melchiahim settlements smell of preserving chemicals and fresh meat. Their lairs are part workshop, part operating theater, part cathedral. Flesh is currency. A fresh corpse is wealth. They maintain complex relationships with mortal populations—sometimes parasitic, sometimes symbiotic (offering protection in exchange for "materials"). Other clans find them repulsive; Melchiahim find other clans naive about the cost of immortality.
- **Philosophy:** *"All flesh is borrowed."* Melchiahim believe that vampiric existence is a continuous act of theft from the living. Some see this as shameful; others see it as honest. What the other clans do to blood, Melchiahim do to the whole body.
- **Territory:** Charnel districts, underground meat markets, flooded catacombs where preservation comes naturally. Their strongholds are built from bone and preserved tissue as much as from stone.

#### Rahabim
The water-dwellers. Rahab's bloodline adapted to an aquatic existence—their skin turned translucent and scaled, their lungs became gills, and their sensitivity to sunlight was replaced by sensitivity to dry air. They inhabit Nosgoth's flooded ruins, submerged cities, and coastal cave systems, controlling territory that other clans cannot even reach.
- **Internal politics:** Rahabim govern through the *Tide Council*—a mobile parliament that convenes at different underwater locations according to a complex tidal calendar known only to the Council's navigators. Decisions are announced through *current-messages*—information carried by manipulated water flows that Rahabim can read by touch. This makes their communication invisible to landlocked races.
- **Coming-of-age:** The *Deep Dive*—a young Rahabim descends to the lowest point of their territory's waters and retrieves an ancestral relic from the dark. The dive tests not just endurance but the ability to navigate by sonar and bioluminescence in absolute blackness. Those who surface with the relic are Named; those who surface without it may try again. Those who do not surface are mourned.
- **Daily life:** Rahabim settlements are submerged or half-flooded—air pockets are maintained for visitors or prisoners, but Rahabim themselves prefer full immersion. Their architecture uses coral, waterlogged stone, and the bones of enormous aquatic creatures. Sound carries differently underwater; Rahabim speech is a complex layering of clicks, hums, and pitched tones that surface-dwellers hear as eerie music.
- **Philosophy:** *"The water remembers what the land forgets."* Rahabim believe that water carries the memory of everything it has touched. Their scholars study currents the way other scholars study books—reading the history of Nosgoth in the flow of its rivers and tides.
- **Territory:** Flooded ruins of the old world, coastal cave networks, and (rumor has it) an entire sunken city deep beneath the Lake of the Dead that the Rahabim have never allowed outsiders to see.
- **Unique vulnerability:** Prolonged exposure to dry air causes Rahabim skin to crack and bleed. They cannot stray far from water without specialized wrappings or alchemical moisturizers. This geographic limitation has made them politically isolated—which suits some Rahabim perfectly and enrages others.

### Inter-Clan Relations

| Clan Pair | Relationship | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Turelim vs. Dumahim | Grudging respect | Both are warrior clans; Turelim find Dumahim silence unnerving, Dumahim find Turelim noise offensive. |
| Zephonim vs. All | Mutual distrust | Every clan assumes Zephonim know their secrets. They're usually right. |
| Melchiahim vs. All | Disgust, pity, or fear | Other clans avoid Melchiahim territory. Melchiahim don't take it personally—they're used to it. |
| Rahabim vs. All | Geographic isolation | Rahabim are so removed from clan politics that some vampires forget they exist. Rahabim prefer it that way. |
| Razielim vs. All | Complicated sympathy | Other clans pity the Razielim while secretly fearing their return. A resurgent Razielim would upend every power structure. |
| Turelim vs. Zephonim | Active hostility | Zephonim infiltration threatens Turelim order. Turelim fortresses are the only structures Zephonim have difficulty penetrating. |

Common threads across all clans:
- Immortality without legacy; sterile and hunted by time.
- Schisms over Kain's choice and the meaning of balance.
- Sanctums in towers, catacombs, and blood-wells; rites of dominion.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A conclave calls a Truce of Nights; sabotage or secure the fragile peace.
	- A Razielim relic stirs the Abyss; retrieve it before a clan war erupts.
	- A blood-tax sparks rebellion in a human quarter; broker terms or break chains.
	- A Melchiahim Flesh Architect has created something unprecedented—a graft that doesn't decay. Every clan wants it. The Architect is asking a price no one wants to pay.
	- A Rahabim current-message surfaces in a landlocked city. It contains coordinates to something the Tide Council has been hiding for centuries. The Rahabim want it back. Badly.
	- A Long Sleeper wakes in Turelim territory and claims authority from an era when their rank was supreme. The current hierarchy disagrees. Both sides are technically correct.
- NPC mentor template: Name (clan epithet) • Role (matriarch/patron) • Quirk (never casts a shadow) • Ask/Price (oath or blood tithe) • Teaches (etiquette, survival politics) • Hooks (renegade brood, cursed vow).

## Wraiths

Souls severed by violent death or unfinished destiny. Many drift to madness; a rare few cling to identity. They phase between realms, drawing strength from memory, emotion, and Soul—half warning, half witness to the Wheel's appetite. Some were once Razielim; others were broken humans or Hylden who escaped the cycle. Without form, they draw power from memory, emotion, and raw Soul energy.

### Origins and Becoming

There is no single path to becoming a Wraith. The most common origins:
- **Violent death** near a planar seam, where the barrier between Material and Spectral is thin enough for a departing soul to snag.
- **Unfinished purpose** so powerful that the soul refuses the Wheel's pull—vengeance, love, a vow spoken at the moment of death.
- **Spectral accident**—caught in the residue of a Soul Reaver's feeding, a Hylden breach, or a failed resurrection ritual.
- **Razielim legacy**—some destroyed Razielim vampires reformed as Wraiths rather than passing on, their spectral nature pulling them into a ghostly half-existence.

### Culture and Customs

Wraiths have no unified society—most are solitary by necessity and temperament. However, loose networks exist:
- **Naming:** Wraiths forget their mortal names over time. They adopt *lament-names*: a word describing their defining emotion or purpose. *Ash-of-Promises*, *The Weeping*, *Last Door*. A Wraith who remembers their mortal name is considered exceptionally grounded—and dangerous, because purpose that strong doesn't fade.
- **Gathering:** Wraiths sometimes cluster at *Echo Wells*—locations where the Spectral Realm is thick enough to sustain conversation. These gatherings are rare, brief, and deeply emotional. Information is exchanged. Warnings are given. Then the Wraiths disperse before spectral predators are drawn to the concentration of Soul energy.
- **The Fading:** Wraiths who lose purpose gradually lose coherence—their form blurs, their memories fragment, and they eventually dissolve into the Spectral Realm's background noise. Other Wraiths call this the *Long Whisper*. It is the Wraith equivalent of dying, and it terrifies them.
- **Anchors:** Most stable Wraiths maintain a physical *anchor*—an object, location, or (rarely) a living person tied to their purpose. If the anchor is destroyed, the Wraith must find a new one within days or begin Fading.

#### Wraith Anchor Rules
*For playable Wraith characters (see also Player's Handbook Ch02 §2.3).*

At character creation, designate one **Anchor** — an object, location, or living person tied to your character's defining purpose or memory. Work with your GM to establish what it is and why it matters.

- **If the Anchor is destroyed or lost:** The Wraith must find and bond to a new Anchor within **7 days** (roughly 1–2 sessions).
- **Each session without an Anchor:** Make a **DR 2 Will check** at the start of the session. On failure, gain **+1 Corruption** as your coherence frays and the Fading begins.
- **Replacing an Anchor:** Requires a short ritual of 1 hour and a meaningful new connection — a place where something significant happened, an object with personal resonance, or a person whose fate the Wraith is genuinely invested in. GMs may require a narrative scene to validate the bond.
- **In the Spectral Realm:** Your Anchor casts a faint spectral echo. Creatures who know the Anchor's location can follow the resonance to find you across planar boundaries.

### Attitudes Toward the Wheel of Fate

Wraiths have the most complex relationship with the Wheel of any lineage. They are *supposed* to be on it but aren't—caught in limbo between the living and the cycled dead. The Elder God considers them defective. Some Wraiths want to rejoin the Wheel (to finally rest). Others refuse it on principle (influenced by Raziel's defiance). A few believe they exist *outside* both the Wheel and the vampire's curse—a third state that neither the Elder God nor Kain's bloodline can claim. These "Free Wraiths" are the most philosophically dangerous beings in Nosgoth.

Common threads:
- Haunted by echoes; empowered by purpose.
- See what mortals miss—planar seams, soul currents, spectral predators.
- Persecuted as omens, courted as guides.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- Lost names whisper from a collapsed mausoleum; carry them to rest.
	- A planar rift feeds on regret; patch it with truth, not stone.
	- A hunted wraith seeks to die properly; grant closure or defy the Wheel.
	- A Wraith's anchor has been stolen by a collector who doesn't understand what they have. The Wraith is Fading. The collector wants to negotiate.
	- An Echo Well has gone silent. The Wraiths who gathered there are gone—not Faded, but *taken*. Something in the Spectral Realm is collecting them.
- NPC mentor template: Name (lament-title) • Role (guide-between) • Quirk (speaks to reflections) • Ask/Price (remember me) • Teaches (safe phasing paths) • Hooks (soul-eater, binding script).

## Humans

Frail yet defiant, humanity endures purges, invasions, and its own zealotry. Adaptive, divided, and unquiet, they swing between faith and survival. Where vampires build dominion, humans build stories—and resistance. They are the last truly mortal race, and the only one still growing.

### Culture and Customs

Human culture in Nosgoth is defined by scarcity and memory. Everything is precious because everything can be taken.

- **Naming:** Human names carry lineage and trade. A Sarafan's name includes their chapter and rank (*Ser Aldric of the Third Purity*). Nomads use parentage and terrain (*Fen, daughter of the Red Wash*). Oracle-Blooded are named by their first vision (*Twice-Seen*, *The Boy Who Knew the Fire*).
- **Economy:** Barter dominates outside Sarafan-controlled zones. Covenant debts—promises of future service—function as currency where goods are scarce. Breaking a covenant debt is social death.
- **Death customs:** Humans burn their dead to prevent necromantic revival. A body left unburned is an insult to the deceased and a danger to the living. Sarafan conduct elaborate funeral pyres with hymns; Nomads wrap the body in memory-threads and burn it at dawn; Oracle-Blooded fast for three days and recite the deceased's last vision.

### Attitudes Toward the Wheel of Fate

Humans are the Wheel's primary inhabitants—their souls cycle through death and rebirth as the cosmic order intends. Most accept this without question. The Sarafan consider it divine law. Nomads see it as a river that carries all things. Oracle-Blooded are the most troubled: their visions sometimes show them the Wheel's machinery, and what they see is not comforting. A growing number of Oracle-Blooded whisper that the Wheel is not a natural cycle but a *feeding mechanism*—though they dare not say for whom.

### Subgroups

#### Sarafan
Zealous knightly orders dedicated to the eradication of vampires. Depending on the era, they are either the ruling military theocracy or scattered, fanatical cells keeping the "purity" laws alive.
- **Internal politics:** The Sarafan are split between *Speakers* (political leadership, diplomats, administrators) and *Swords* (military commanders, inquisitors, field operatives). The two factions need each other but despise each other's methods. Speakers think Swords are brutes; Swords think Speakers are cowards.
- **Daily life:** Sarafan territory is ordered, clean, and thoroughly surveilled. Curfews are enforced. Vampire-adjacent activities (blood magic, necromancy, even studying vampiric history) are punishable by imprisonment or death. Citizens are safe but unfree.
- **The Purity Laws:** A Sarafan's greatest fear is corruption—not just vampiric, but moral. The Purity Laws demand confession, regular spiritual examination, and the reporting of "deviant thought." This creates a society where trust is constant performance and privacy is suspicious.

#### Nomads
Refusers of thrones and temples. Roaming reclamationists who map wildlands and barter with ruins.
- **Internal politics:** Nomad bands are led by *Path-Speakers*—elders chosen for their knowledge of routes, ruins, and survival. Succession is by acclaim, not blood. A Path-Speaker who leads their band into danger and cannot lead them out is replaced without ceremony.
- **The Memory-Threads:** In a world where history is a weapon, Nomads trust no written word. They are keepers of *Memory-Threads*—a complex oral and artisanal tradition. Each tribe's history is woven into massive, intricate tapestries where the color of a thread, the tightness of a knot, and the pattern of a weave tells the "true" story of Nosgoth. These *Lore-Tapestries* are read by specially trained *Thread-Singers*, whose ritual chants are said to resonate with the world's memory, bypassing Moebius's temporal manipulations. To see a Nomad's tapestry is to see a history free of the Time-Streamer's lies—a truth so dangerous that the Sarafan hunt them as heretics and Moebius's agents erase them from existence wherever they are found.
- **Daily life:** Nomads travel in bands of 20-60, following seasonal routes through Nosgoth's wildlands. They trade in salvage, knowledge, and safe passage. Their camps are deliberately temporary—nothing permanent, nothing that can be taken. They are the only humans who regularly interact with Melchiahim (trading preserved corpses for protection) and occasionally with Rahabim (trading surface news for underwater passage).

#### Oracle-Blooded
Born under omens, touched by the whispers of Moebius or the Time Stream. Seers and healers feared for their foresight.
- **Internal politics:** Oracle-Blooded have no formal organization—their gift is too rare and too personal. However, a loose network called the *Sight Chain* connects them: when one Oracle-Blooded has a vision concerning another, they are obligated to share it. This creates involuntary bonds of trust and obligation between people who may never meet.
- **The Burden of Sight:** An Oracle-Blooded's visions are not a gift—they are an intrusion. Visions arrive uninvited: while eating, sleeping, mid-conversation. They show fragments of possible futures, usually the worst ones. Most Oracle-Blooded develop coping mechanisms: some journal obsessively, some drink, some learn to lie about what they've seen.
- **Daily life:** Oracle-Blooded who are public about their gift become community assets—and community prisoners. People come to them for guidance, for warnings, for hope. They are consulted before harvests, marriages, and battles. The ones who refuse to share their visions are called *Blind Seers*—feared for what they're hiding.

Common threads:
- Barter economies and covenant debts.
- Bitter memory of the vampire ascendancy and Hylden wars.
- Survival through craft, faith, and stubborn kinship.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A Sarafan reliquary goes missing; return it before it becomes a war-banner.
	- A Nomad map shows a moving oasis; it's a living creature.
	- An Oracle-Blooded predicts your doom—unless you defy three small fates.
	- A Thread-Singer has been captured by the Sarafan. The Lore-Tapestry they carry contains a history the Sarafan need to remain hidden. The Nomads want their elder back. The Sarafan want the tapestry burned.
	- The Sight Chain has gone dark. Oracle-Blooded across Nosgoth have independently had the same vision: a door opening in the sky and something looking through. None of them will describe what they saw on the other side.
- NPC mentor template: Name (trade-name or title) • Role (elder, captain, seer) • Quirk (keeps tally knots) • Ask/Price (service rendered) • Teaches (routes, contacts, rites) • Hooks (faction debt, false prophecy).

## Hylden-Blooded

Mortals reshaped by the banished race's alien influence—through artifacts, rituals, whispers, or experiments. The corruption marks flesh and mind: glyph scarring, bio-mechanical growths, eyes reflecting the void. Power arrives hand-in-hand with the erosion of self.

### Origins and the Mark

The Hylden-Blooded are not born—they are *made*. The transformation can be sudden (a single artifact exposure) or gradual (years of Hylden whisper-contact), but the result is always the same: the person they were begins to dissolve, replaced by something that thinks in geometries and speaks in frequencies.

- **The Mark:** Every Hylden-Blooded bears a visible sign of corruption—glowing glyph-lines beneath the skin, bio-mechanical growths that pulse with alien rhythm, eyes that reflect light from a different spectrum. The Mark cannot be hidden permanently; any attempt to conceal it eventually fails as the corruption reasserts itself.
- **The Whispers:** Most Hylden-Blooded hear fragments of Hylden communication—equations, arguments, directives—leaking from the exile dimension. Some can filter these into useful information. Others cannot stop listening. The distinction between the two determines whether a Hylden-Blooded functions in society or goes mad.

### Culture and Customs

Hylden-Blooded lack a unified culture—they are anomalies, not a people. However, survival creates its own traditions:
- **Naming:** Many abandon their mortal names and adopt *sigil-names*—the glyph that first appeared on their body, translated into a spoken word. *Fracture*, *Null-Thread*, *The Seventh Angle*. Others stubbornly keep their mortal names as an act of resistance against the change.
- **The Void Circle:** Loose networks of Hylden-Blooded who share knowledge about managing corruption, interpreting whispers, and surviving the prejudice of both human and vampire societies. Meetings are rare and paranoid. Trust is earned through shared scars.
- **Corruption Management:** Hylden-Blooded have developed personal rituals to slow their transformation—meditation, controlled glyph-discharge, deliberate exposure to "counter-frequencies" (sounds and patterns that disrupt Hylden influence). None of these work perfectly. All buy time.

### Attitudes Toward the Wheel of Fate

Hylden-Blooded occupy a deeply unsettled position. Their mortal souls are still on the Wheel, but the Hylden influence pulling at them is fundamentally anti-Wheel—the Hylden's original war was against the cosmic order that created it. Some Hylden-Blooded feel their soul being pulled in two directions simultaneously. This is not metaphorical. Wraiths who observe Hylden-Blooded in the Spectral Realm report seeing their soul stretched between two points, vibrating like a plucked string.

Common threads:
- Fragmented directives echoing from the exile plane.
- Mastery of glyphcasting and forbidden arts at a terrible price.
- Feared by humans, distrusted by vampires, coveted by conspirators.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A glyph-iron growth spreads through a ward; amputate the district or cure it.
	- Whispers promise a cure for corruption; it's a trap set by a Remnant.
	- A Hylden engine needs a conscience; whose soul would you wire in?
	- A Void Circle meeting is interrupted by Sarafan raiders. The Hylden-Blooded who were present scatter—but one of them was carrying a device that translates the Whispers into readable text. Both the Sarafan and the Hylden Remnants want it.
	- A Hylden-Blooded has stopped hearing the Whispers. Completely. This has never happened before. They don't know if they're cured or if the silence means something worse.
- NPC mentor template: Name (sigil-name) • Role (defector scholar) • Quirk (writes on skin) • Ask/Price (artifact shard) • Teaches (risks, counters) • Hooks (exile hunters, living machine).

## Revenants

Corpse-bound souls animated by sheer will or mistake—half-wraith, half-corpse. They rot, yet remember. Their second life is a contract with purpose: vengeance unfed, debts unpaid, vows unfulfilled.

### Origins and the Binding

Revenants arise when the conditions for death are met but the conditions for departure are not. The most common triggers:
- **A vow spoken at the moment of death** that the Wheel cannot process—the soul refuses to cycle until the vow is fulfilled.
- **Necromantic interference**—a soul pulled back before the Wheel claims it, bound to its original body by a practitioner's magic. These Revenants often resent their resurrector.
- **Divine error**—extremely rare cases where the soul simply does not leave. No one knows why. Not the Wheel, not the Elder God, not the Revenant. They just... stayed.

### Culture and Customs

Revenants exist in a social no-man's-land. They are neither alive nor properly dead, and both the living and the dead find them unsettling.

- **Naming:** Revenants keep their mortal names but add a *grave-title*—a description of their unfinished business. *Aldric the Owed*, *Mira of the Broken Promise*, *The One Who Saw the Knife*. When the business is completed, the grave-title is dropped—and the Revenant usually follows shortly after.
- **The Purpose:** Every Revenant has one—a single driving imperative that keeps their soul anchored to their decaying body. The Purpose is usually obvious (avenge my murder, protect my family, deliver this message). Occasionally it is obscure, and the Revenant spends their second life trying to figure out *why they came back*. These "Lost Purpose" Revenants are the most unpredictable.
- **Physical decay:** Revenants rot. Slowly, but consistently. Their body is maintained by will, not biology. The more focused a Revenant's Purpose, the slower the decay. Distracted or confused Revenants crumble faster. Most develop a pragmatic relationship with their deterioration—patching holes, binding loosened limbs, and accepting that aesthetics are no longer a priority.
- **The Second Death:** When a Revenant's Purpose is fulfilled, the animating will departs and the body collapses. This is considered a *good* Second Death—clean, purposeful, earned. A *bad* Second Death occurs when the Purpose becomes impossible to fulfill. The Revenant's will erodes, their body fails, and they disintegrate into something that is neither soul nor corpse—a *remnant echo* that haunts the location of their failure.

### Attitudes Toward the Wheel of Fate

Revenants are the Wheel's error messages. They should have cycled through death and rebirth, but their Purpose held them. The Elder God considers them an affront—souls it cannot claim. Revenants who are aware of the Wheel tend to view it with weary resignation: "I'll get on when I'm done." Some Revenants discover they don't *want* to get on—their second life has given them more clarity, more meaning, than their first. The question of whether a Revenant can choose to keep existing after their Purpose is fulfilled is one of Nosgoth's most disturbing theological puzzles.
*Hash'ak'gik's Vessels—mortals hollowed out and rebuilt as demon conduits—present a specific threat to Revenants: a Vessel who encounters a Revenant may recognize their half-spectral nature and attempt to use them as an anchor or amplifier for Hash'ak'gik’s influence. Revenants are neither fully living nor fully dead, and the demon finds this ambiguity useful. Revenants operating near Avernus territory or in regions where Cult cells are active should be aware that they may be perceived as resources, not enemies.*
Common threads:
- Unfinished business that bends fate around them.
- Resisted by sanctified ground; tolerated by pragmatists.
- Stories of guardianship, obsession, and tragic clarity.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A revenant guards a bridge until a promise is kept; define the promise.
	- A necromancer sells second chances; collect the bill.
	- A saint's bones refuse interment; discover who still needs forgiveness.
	- A Revenant has completed their Purpose but hasn't collapsed. They are still here. They don't know why. Neither does anyone else. The Wardens are concerned. The Elder God's cult is furious.
	- A "Lost Purpose" Revenant has been wandering for decades, leaving ash-prints through three territories. Their path, when mapped, traces the outline of a glyph. What happens when the glyph is complete?
- NPC mentor template: Name (grave-name) • Role (watchman or oath-keeper) • Quirk (leaves ash prints) • Ask/Price (finish this) • Teaches (oath-binding, remembrance) • Hooks (wronged village, stolen vow).

## Unbound

Anomalies born of temporal misfires and metaphysical ruptures. The Unbound defy prophecy and refuse the Wheel's grammar. Luck and timing warp around them—glitches in fate that no oracle can chart.

### Origins and the Fracture

The leading theory among scholars: the Unbound were created at the exact moment Kain refused the Sacrifice at the Pillars. That single act of defiance cracked the timeline, and the Unbound are the splinters—*splinter-souls* that exist outside the Wheel of Fate because the Wheel broke around them. They are living paradoxes, proof that destiny can be hacked.

Other theories exist:
- **Elder God shards**—fragments of the cosmic parasite's consciousness that accidentally achieved independence.
- **Temporal echoes**—people from timelines that no longer exist, stranded in the current one with no past and no predetermined future.
- **Random noise**—the universe simply making mistakes, and the Unbound are those mistakes given legs.

No one knows which theory is correct. The Unbound don't care. They just know the rules don't apply to them—and that makes everyone else nervous.

### Culture and Customs

Unbound have no unified culture because they have no unified origin. They find each other occasionally, usually by accident (the probability warps around them tend to attract similar anomalies).

- **Naming:** Unbound names are often impossible titles or paradoxes. *The Door That Was Already Open*, *Never-Once*, *Coin Landing On Its Edge*. Some use perfectly ordinary names as an act of defiance against the absurdity of their existence.
- **The Coincidence:** When two Unbound meet, improbable things happen in their vicinity—objects move, weather shifts, people nearby experience sudden memories that aren't theirs. Unbound call this *The Coincidence* and treat it as a greeting. If The Coincidence doesn't happen, one of them isn't actually Unbound.
- **Probability Hygiene:** Experienced Unbound learn to manage the probability distortions around them. An unmanaged Unbound is a walking disaster area—floorboards break, fires start, locks open. A managed Unbound can *direct* the distortions, using them as an intuitive form of reality manipulation. The difference between the two is practice, will, and a hard-earned sense of humor.

### Attitudes Toward the Wheel of Fate

The Unbound are the Wheel's blind spot. Their souls cannot be tracked, predicted, or claimed by the Elder God. This makes them:
- **Invisible to the Elder God:** It cannot perceive them, which means it cannot consume their souls. This enrages it beyond measure.
- **Immune to prophecy:** No oracle can see their future. No Time-Streamer can manipulate their timeline. They walk through destiny's web without causing a single thread to vibrate.
- **Extremely valuable:** Every faction wants Unbound operatives—agents who cannot be predicted, scried, or fated. The Pale Accord covets them. The Sarafan fear them. The Wardens of Balance are theologically tormented by their existence.
- **Profoundly lonely:** No one who exists outside the pattern truly belongs anywhere. Unbound relationships are intense but fragile—they know the universe has no plan for them, which means the universe has no plan for their connections, either.

Common threads:
- Immune to easy categorization and control.
- Magnetize accidents, opportunities, and paradoxes.
- Revered as proof of freedom; reviled as insults to order.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A prophecy stalls on your presence; restart or rewrite the world's line.
	- A time-thief wants to buy your “unused moments.”
	- A paradox child vanishes in places it has not yet been; track a future.
	- Moebius's Chronoplast cannot map you. His followers consider this blasphemy. They want you corrected—or erased. The question is whether "correction" means killing you or figuring out how to put you *back on* the Wheel.
	- Two Unbound have met in the same city and The Coincidence is escalating. Buildings are shifting position. People are remembering things that haven't happened. If a third Unbound arrives, the probability distortion might become permanent—a zone of Nosgoth where fate simply doesn't function.
- NPC mentor template: Name (impossible title) • Role (wanderer anomaly) • Quirk (never appears to use doors) • Ask/Price (one coin flipped later) • Teaches (dodging fate) • Hooks (Warden inquiry, Elder attention).

## Inter-Lineage Relations

The lineages of Nosgoth do not exist in isolation—their histories are tangled, their prejudices deep, and their alliances fragile. GMs can use this table to generate instant social friction when characters of different lineages interact.

| Lineage A | Lineage B | Default Relationship | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Vampires | Humans | Predator/prey, master/servant | Centuries of exploitation have created a well of hatred that individual kindness cannot easily drain. |
| Vampires | Wraiths | Wariness | Wraiths can see vampire souls (or the lack thereof). Vampires find this deeply uncomfortable. |
| Vampires | Hylden-Blooded | Mutual distrust | The Hylden are an ancient enemy; their Blooded carry that taint. But some clans will use any tool. |
| Vampires | Revenants | Grudging respect | Revenants' refusal to die mirrors the vampire condition. Both understand persistence. |
| Vampires | Unbound | Fascination | A being outside fate is useful to immortals tired of cosmic manipulation. |
| Humans | Wraiths | Fear, superstition | Most humans see Wraiths as omens of death. Some recognize them as guides. |
| Humans | Hylden-Blooded | Horror, pity, revulsion | Hylden-Blooded were once human. That makes them worse, not better, in most human eyes. |
| Humans | Revenants | Complex | Revenants were human. Some communities welcome them back; others see a corpse wearing a friend's face. |
| Humans | Unbound | Unease | Something is *wrong* about the Unbound. Humans can't articulate it, but they feel it. |
| Wraiths | Hylden-Blooded | Mutual avoidance | Both exist in states of metaphysical distortion. Neither wants to find out what happens when those distortions overlap. |
| Wraiths | Revenants | Sympathy | Both refused death. The difference is that Wraiths left their bodies and Revenants didn't. |
| Wraiths | Unbound | Confusion | Wraiths can perceive everyone's place in the cosmic pattern except the Unbound's. The blank space is unnerving. |
| Hylden-Blooded | Revenants | Indifference | Both are outcasts. Neither has the energy for additional prejudice. |
| Hylden-Blooded | Unbound | Cautious interest | Hylden-Blooded hear whispers from outside; Unbound exist outside. There might be a connection. |
| Revenants | Unbound | Philosophical kinship | Both defy the expected order. Revenants defy death; Unbound defy fate. They understand each other. |

## Lineage Goals and Core Problems

Use this table to quickly establish a lineage-specific motivation or complication for a PC or NPC.

| Lineage | Core Problem | Player Goal |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Razielim | Near-extinction | Survive; find a cure or a path forward |
| Turelim | Crumbling authority as other clans rise | Maintain legacy; adapt or fight |
| Dumahim | Apathy and silence of their fallen clan | Re-ignite will and forge a future |
| Zephonim | Isolation threatened by a covetous world | Protect secrets; control information |
| Melchiahim | Bodily decay accelerating beyond control | Find a means to slow or reverse it |
| Rahabim | Cut off from vampire politics by geography | Assert relevance; claim territory |
| Wraiths | Losing coherence; the Fading | Find an anchor; fulfill purpose before dissolution |
| Sarafan | Zealotry consuming their own people | Reform from within or break free |
| Nomads | Knowledge preservation under persecution | Keep the Memory-Threads alive; find safe ground |
| Oracle-Blooded | Visions that isolate and endanger | Control the gift; decide what to share |
| Hylden-Blooded | Transformation eroding identity | Manage corruption; find community |
| Revenants | Purpose completion means true death | Decide whether to finish what they started |
| Unbound | Existence without cosmic context | Find belonging in a world built on patterns they break |
