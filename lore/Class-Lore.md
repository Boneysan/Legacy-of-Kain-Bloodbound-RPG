## Blood Knight

Warriors dedicated to the art of vascular combat. Historically, the most famous Blood Knights were Vorador’s disciples or the elite guard of the Turelim clan. They view battle as a sacrament, channeling their own vitality—or their enemy’s—to fuel supernatural resilience. In the current era, they are mercenaries or champions of the independent vampire covens.

Common threads:
- Origins in vampiric blood-cults or as survivors of the arena pits.
- Code of honor centered on strength and the “purity” of combat.
- Mastery of blood-magic infusions; Turelim-lineaged Blood Knights also exhibit a residual telekinetic touch—a heritage trait, not a trained school—that manifests as an ambient pressure of force in the heat of battle.

### Roleplay Guidance

Blood Knights are creatures of conviction. Where Dreadblades calculate, Blood Knights commit—fully, visibly, and without hesitation. They do not consider retreat. They do not accept healing they didn’t earn. To a Blood Knight, the body is both weapon and testament: the scars they carry are the only résumé that matters.

- **Voice and manner:** Blunt and declarative. Does not soften statements or defer to social niceties. Speaks of past battles as if they were sacred texts. Prone to long silences followed by single-sentence verdicts.
- **Social habits:** Respects strength above rank. More comfortable in the fighting pit than the throne room, but will stand at the throne room door as long as someone worthy sits inside. Forms opinions about everyone’s fighting stance before knowing their name.
- **Fears:** Dying without having tested themselves against a worthy opponent. Being healed by someone they didn’t ask. Becoming irrelevant.

### Factional Ties
- **Vampire Clans:** Blood Knights find natural homes in the Turelim and Dumahim broods, who value martial discipline and endurance. Vorador’s tradition treats them as honored students of an older school. Kainite courts employ them as champions.
- **Crimson Fracture:** Deeply conflicted relationship. The Fracture’s philosophy of “purity in predation” appeals to some Blood Knights’ codes, but the Fracture’s embrace of uncontrolled frenzy violates the Blood Knight’s core ethic of *disciplined* strength.
- **Sarafan Order:** Mutual hostility, but also grudging respect in individual combat. A Sarafan Knight-Confessor and a Blood Knight may despise each other’s existence while still recognizing the other as a genuine warrior. This creates complicated situations when combat honor demands fair terms.
- **Outlands Guilds:** Blood Knights are in consistent demand as guards, champions, and problem-solvers. The Red Ledger employs several as enforcers. The Guilds pay well and ask no political questions.

### Rites and Traditions
- **The Blood Sacrament:** Before a significant battle, a Blood Knight opens a shallow wound on their sword-arm and lets blood fall to the earth. It is an offering and a promise—“I bleed willingly before the blade bleeds me.” A Blood Knight who skips this rite before battle is considered either arrogant or desperate.
- **The Reckoning:** When a Blood Knight is defeated in honorable combat, they owe the victor a *reckoning*—one service that cannot be refused, within the bounds of honor. Reckoning debts are tracked in carving on the sword-hilt. A hilt without carvings belongs to a knight who has never been bested—or one who has never fought honestly.
- **The Last Sip:** A Blood Knight’s death rite. When a Blood Knight consents to end, their blood is collected in a ritual vessel by those who witnessed their final battle. It is consumed by the next youngest Blood Knight in their lineage—not to feed, but to *remember*. The tradition holds that the strength of every previous knight is carried in the line.

### Sacred Sites
- **The Arena of Vorador’s First Kill:** A crumbling amphitheater hidden in the ruins of what was once Vorador’s outlying estate, where the elder vampire reportedly first mastered blade-and-blood in tandem. Blood Knights treat this as pilgrimage: they fight three bouts here—against a worthy opponent, against their own fear, and against a memory they believe they have mastered. Only when all three are honest does the pilgrimage count.
- **The Red Vault:** A subterranean armory beneath a Turelim stronghold, said to hold weapon-relics from champions who earned the right to retire their blades. Weapons are displayed without owners’ names—only the scars they carry, for identification. To deposit your blade in the Red Vault is to acknowledge the weapon outgrew you. To retrieve one is to claim kinship with a lineage you must then justify.

### Famous Blood Knights
- **Vorador’s Champion (unnamed, pre-Kain era):** A Blood Knight who served as Vorador’s personal guard and, according to fragmentary accounts, trained Vorador’s earliest disciples. Name lost to history. Legacy survives in a fighting style called *the Voradori Press*—three feints, one real cut, all fueled by a blood-offering made mid-sequence.
- **Kaetha of the Turelim:** A Turelim Blood Knight who challenged three consecutive clan champions and won all three before being exiled for “excessive honor”—she had refused to kill a defeated opponent who asked to surrender, which the council considered weakness. She now teaches blade-rites to outcasts and those the clans have discarded.

### Associated Relics
- **The Vitality Crest:** A medallion inscribed with radiating blade-lines, worn over the sternum by senior Blood Knights. It does not grant power, but *registers* it—subtle warmth when the wearer is at peak combat vitality, cold when they are compromised. Experienced Blood Knights read a companion’s Crest-temperature at a glance.
- **The Thirsty Blade:** A broad-curved blade that does not retain blood on its edge—it consumes blood into the metal. Blood Knights who use it report the blade growing heavier and easier to swing simultaneously, as if it has opinions about worthy opponents.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A Blood Rite arena opens for the first time in decades; the champion’s sigil matches a PC’s forgotten lineage.
	- A war-host marches to “purify” a ruin; the only way to avert massacre is ritual combat by proxy.
	- A rival knight carries a relic blade that drinks vows; breaking it requires an oath freely forsaken.
	- The Red Vault has been breached: someone is stealing weapons by name, targeting specific lineages. Who knows these names—and why do they want that legacy dead?
	- Kaetha of the Turelim asks the party to witness a formal challenge she has no hope of winning. She’s not trying to win. She’s trying to make a point. What point—and who is the real audience?
- NPC mentor template: Name (old honorific) • Role (duelist-priest or fortress captain) • Quirk (never removes gauntlets) • Ask/Price (sworn promise or service) • Teaches (martial conduct, dueling codes) • Hooks (feud, heretical rite, lost squire).

## Soul Reaver

Few dare to wield the echoes of the Reaver blade’s legacy. Born of Raziel’s fragmented memory and the path between realms, Soul Reavers phase through worlds and strike at body and spirit alike. Each is haunted by visions of possible futures—and pasts. Rare and feared, they’re often hunted by both Sarafan and Hylden for what they might become.

Common threads:
- Walkers-between-worlds guided by spectral premonition.
- Strive to reconcile fate’s echo with their own will.
- Pursued by fanatics and conspirators who covet or fear Reaver lineage.
> **Becoming a Soul Reaver: In-Game Origin Pathway**
> The Soul Reaver class is intentionally disconnected from lineage—any race can become one. While a player may begin as a Soul Reaver at character creation, the class also supports mid-campaign transformation. A character of any class who dies and is resurrected under specific soul-scarring circumstances may become a Soul Reaver:
> - **Consumed by the Abyss** and returned—the Abyss does not kill; it *remakes*.
> - **Dying near a shattered Pillar**, where the boundary between realms is thinnest and a departing soul can snag on the broken metaphysics.
> - **Surviving a Soul Reaver's feeding**—the spectral resonance lingers, rewriting the victim's relationship with both realms.
> - **Failed resurrection or necromantic ritual** that tears the soul loose but doesn't fully anchor it in either realm.
>
> GMs should treat this as a major story event, not a casual multiclass. The character loses their previous class abilities over 1–2 sessions as the spectral transformation takes hold, gaining Soul Reaver abilities in their place. See GM Guide Chapter 3 (Character Progression) for mechanical guidance.
### Roleplay Guidance

Soul Reavers live in two worlds simultaneously and belong fully to neither. They are introspective to the point of seeming distracted—because they *are* distracted, by visions, echoes, and the constant hum of the Spectral Realm at the edge of perception. They question everything, trust slowly, and carry a bone-deep awareness that their existence is a paradox.

- **Voice and manner:** Thoughtful, measured. Pauses mid-sentence as if listening to something no one else can hear. Often speaks in conditional tense: *"If this is the timeline where..."* or *"Assuming we're still in the same present..."*
- **Social habits:** Prefer small groups. Deeply loyal to those who accept them without fear. Drawn to liminal spaces—doorways, bridges, shores, twilight.
- **Fears:** Being trapped in one realm permanently. Being used as a tool by forces that see them as a key rather than a person.

### Factional Ties
- **Razielim:** Soul Reavers are regarded with reverence and suspicion by surviving Razielim, who see them as inheritors of their progenitor's mantle.
- **Sarafan Order:** Actively hunted. The Sarafan consider Soul Reavers proof of vampiric corruption and spectral heresy.
- **Wardens of Balance:** Tense respect. Wardens recognize Soul Reavers as essential to planar stability but fear their potential for disruption.
- **Crimson Fracture:** Desperately coveted. The Fracture believes Soul Reavers hold the secret to merging the realms.

### Rites and Traditions
- **The Walk Between:** A Soul Reaver's initiation requires phasing into the Spectral Realm without guidance, surviving for a full night, and returning with a *truth-stone*—a pebble from the Spectral that retains its otherworldly blue glow in the Material. The stone is carried always, a proof of passage.
- **The Echo Vigil:** Before making any decision of lasting consequence, a Soul Reaver enters a trance to consult the echoes—spectral impressions of past events at the site of the decision. The visions are unreliable, contradictory, and sometimes terrifying. That's the point.
- **Mourning the Unborn:** Soul Reavers mourn futures that never happened. When a companion dies, they spend a night phased, witnessing the spectral echo of the life that *might* have been. It is considered the truest form of grief.

### Sacred Sites
- **The Shattered Threshold:** A rift where the Material and Spectral Realms overlap permanently, located in the canyons near the Abyss. Soul Reavers make pilgrimage here to test their phasing abilities and commune with spectral entities. The air shimmers; compasses spin.
- **Raziel's Echo Chamber:** A cave system where Raziel's spectral imprint lingers strongest. Visitors report hearing fragments of his thoughts—questions without answers, rage without target, sorrow without end.

### Famous Soul Reavers
- **Thane of the Shattered Threshold:** A Soul Reaver who has lived at the rift for decades, guiding new Reavers through their first phasing. He is ancient, kind, and wrong about almost everything—but his mistakes are instructive.
- **Echalis the Unresolved:** A young Soul Reaver who experiences future echoes so vivid they paralyze her. She has not spoken in three months. When she moves, she moves with absolute precision—as if she's already seen the outcome.

### Associated Relics
- **The Truth-Stone of First Passage:** The original truth-stone, said to have been carried by the first Soul Reaver to survive the Walk Between. It glows brighter in the presence of lies and dims near honest speech.
- **The Veil-Cloak:** A mantle woven from spectral silk that eases the transition between realms—reducing the soul-stress of phasing and making the wearer partially translucent even in the Material.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- Echoes show two mutually exclusive futures; the Reaver must choose which ruin stands.
	- Sarafan archivists bait a trap with a false prophecy that only a Reaver can contradict.
	- A soul-storm sweeps the region; only phasing rituals can rescue the trapped.
	- Raziel's Echo Chamber has gone silent. Something is consuming the echoes—and growing fat on them.
	- A Soul Reaver's truth-stone has turned black. What lie has become so pervasive that it's poisoned the stone?
- NPC mentor template: Name (whispered epithet) • Role (spectral guide or blade-keeper) • Quirk (answers in paradox) • Ask/Price (carry a message across realms) • Teaches (phasing taboos, reading echoes) • Hooks (sealed gate, hunted by Wardens).

## Shadowmancer

Masters of stealth and illusion, often tracing their techniques back to the spider-clan of Zephon. They manipulate shadows not just to hide, but as physical weapons. In the fractured world, they serve as spies for the high clans or assassins for the highest bidder. Their connection to the darkness is almost symbiotic.

Common threads:
- Legacies in Zephonim rites or the training of human thieves' guilds.
- Focus on verticality, silence, and the psychological impact of fear.
- Often adorned with insectoid or chitinous motifs in homage to their progenitor.

### Roleplay Guidance

Shadowmancers are patient, observant, and deeply uncomfortable being observed. They do not enter rooms—they appear in them. They don't leave conversations—they were never fully in them. Every Shadowmancer maintains at least one false identity, and the best ones have forgotten which name is real.

- **Voice and manner:** Quiet, precise. Never raises their voice; when they whisper, people lean in. Tends to stand where the light doesn't reach. Eyes track exits before people.
- **Social habits:** Collect secrets the way others collect coins. Prefer to know three things about every person in a room before speaking. Trust is given in droplets, withdrawn in floods.
- **Fears:** Exposure. Not death or pain—being *seen*, truly, with nowhere to hide. A Shadowmancer's worst nightmare is a well-lit room with no doors.

### Factional Ties
- **Zephonim Clan:** The ancestral patrons. Many Shadowmancers train in Zephonim vertical lairs, learning to move on walls and ceilings before they learn to walk properly on floors.
- **Outlands Guilds:** The Hollowmen smuggling guild and the Tongues information brokers employ Shadowmancers extensively. The pay is silence; the pension is continued breathing.
- **Sarafan Order:** Shadowmancers who once served the Sarafan as "Veil Operatives" were quietly purged when the Order decided secrecy was too close to heresy. Survivors hold grudges.
- **Pale Accord:** Shadowmancers make excellent field agents for the Accord's clandestine research operations.

### Rites and Traditions
- **The Naming of Shadows:** Upon completing training, a Shadowmancer chooses a shadow-name—a title used only in professional contexts. Revealing a Shadowmancer's birth name is an act of war.
- **The Veil-Test:** The final examination: the initiate must enter a crowded market, steal a specific object from a specific person, and return it to the guildmaster—all without being seen *or remembered*. The object is always trivial. The lesson is that value is what you define it to be.
- **The Dark Toast:** Shadowmancers drink alone but make a toast to "the one who watches"—an acknowledgment that no matter how good you are, someone is better. It keeps them humble. Mostly.

### Sacred Sites
- **The Web of Zephon:** A vast network of silk-like shadow-threads strung through an abandoned cathedral tower. The threads vibrate with information—footsteps, whispers, the opening of doors miles away. Only Shadowmancers trained in Zephonim techniques can read them.
- **The Blackout Market:** Not a fixed location but a concept—a market that exists only in total darkness, moving between ruined basements and sealed caves. Goods are exchanged by touch and description alone. Prices are spoken, never written.

### Famous Shadowmancers
- **"Nobody":** The most famous Shadowmancer is someone no one can describe, name, or prove exists. Attributed with seventeen assassinations, the theft of a Pillar-shard, and the infiltration of the Elder God's cult. May be one person, several people, or a cautionary tale that got out of hand.
- **Silk-of-Zephon:** A Zephonim vampire Shadowmancer who weaves literal spider-silk veils that induce hallucinations in anyone who touches them. She runs a spy network from a tower so vertical that humans cannot climb it.

### Associated Relics
- **The Cloak of Absent Presence:** A garment that doesn't make the wearer invisible—it makes observers *forget to look*. Witnesses recall a vague shape, nothing more.
- **Zephon's Thread:** A single strand of silk from the progenitor's lair. When stretched between two points, it transmits sound perfectly across any distance. Spymasters would commit atrocities for this.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A tower's shadow moves against the stars—someone is weaving a city-sized veil.
	- A client hires the party to stage a haunting that becomes all too real.
	- A stolen mask lets a petty noble command darkness; retrieve or replace it with a safer decoy.
	- "Nobody" has been sighted—or rather, unsighted. Three different people claim to have seen nothing in three different cities on the same night. Something is being erased.
	- The Web of Zephon is vibrating with a message no one sent. Something is using the network to communicate—from the other side.
- NPC mentor template: Name (many aliases) • Role (spymaster or veilmage) • Quirk (never stands in direct light) • Ask/Price (one secret for another) • Teaches (misdirection crafts) • Hooks (rival cell, cursed disguise).

## Sangromancer

Heirs to Nosgoth’s darkest magical legacy, Sangromancers practice blood sorcery. They blend Hylden rites, vampiric biology, and forbidden alchemy to reshape flesh and seize the battlefield. Banned by the Sarafan and feared by the Balance Wardens, many hide in ruined temples and blood-wells, their power rising with every drop spilled—and with an intimate sense of the living pulse of the world.

Common threads:
- Taboos broken in pursuit of living magic.
- Hermetic circles in catacombs, cisterns, and old sanctums.
- Feared healers and feared killers—two edges of the same blade.

### Roleplay Guidance

Sangromancers see the world as a circulatory system—connections, flows, pressures. They are simultaneously the most intimate and the most clinical of Nosgoth's practitioners. A Sangromancer knows your blood type by scent, your health by the color of your veins, and your emotional state by your pulse. This makes them extraordinary healers and terrifying enemies.

- **Voice and manner:** Clinically precise. Speaks about bodies the way an architect speaks about buildings—structurally, without embarrassment. Often touches people's wrists uninvited to "take a reading."
- **Social habits:** Prefer small, trusted circles (pun intended). Keep detailed journals of biological observations. Eat sparingly and drink water obsessively—"to keep the channels clear."
- **Fears:** Contamination—not physical, but magical. A Sangromancer fears having their blood-sorcery corrupted into something they can't control, turning them into the very plague they were trying to cure.

### Factional Ties
- **Sarafan Order:** Active persecution. The Sarafan consider blood sorcery the ultimate heresy—"the Hylden's grammar written in human ink."
- **Vampire Clans:** Complex relationship. Vampires respect the power but fear the intimacy—a Sangromancer who understands your blood understands your weaknesses.
- **Pale Accord:** The Accord's Chapter of States includes several Sangromancers studying the transformation of matter through blood. It's the closest thing to an institutional home.
- **Outlands Guilds:** Sangromancers who work as battlefield medics or plague-doctors are valued by the guilds, who ask few questions about methodology.

### Rites and Traditions
- **The Red Communion:** Initiation requires the aspirant to heal a mortal wound using only their blood-magic—no tools, no assistants, no preparation. The wound is inflicted on the aspirant themselves. If they survive their own healing, they are a Sangromancer.
- **The Pulse Circle:** Before major workings, Sangromancers join hands and synchronize heartbeats through blood-resonance. The resulting unified pulse amplifies their magic. Outsiders who witness the ritual report hearing a single, massive heartbeat in the walls.
- **The Offering of Drops:** A Sangromancer never takes blood without offering their own first. It is a code of reciprocity—"I will not ask of your veins what I would not ask of mine." Breaking this code is considered not just unethical but technically dangerous, as unreciprocated blood-magic tends to rebound.

### Sacred Sites
- **The Blood-Well of Avernus:** A deep cistern beneath the ruins of Avernus Cathedral, where blood-magic was first codified by Hylden researchers using Janos Audron's stolen heart as a power source. The well still pulses with residual energy. Sangromancers who meditate here can sense the heartbeats of every living creature within a mile. *Note: the Blood-Well predates the Cult of Hash'ak'gik by centuries, but the Cult now controls the upper cathedral and the primary access tunnels above it. Any Sangromancer pilgrimage must pass through active Hash'ak'gik-held territory. This is not optional; it is the price of the Well.*
- **The Red Library:** A hidden archive where blood-sorcery texts are inscribed on vellum made from donated skin. The books are alive in a sense—they warm to the touch and their ink shifts in response to the reader's vitality.

### Famous Sangromancers
- **Vithari the Twice-Bled:** A Sangromancer who survived the Red Communion twice—once as a human, once after being turned. She claims to understand both mortal and immortal circulatory systems, and her knowledge is in terrifying demand.
- **The Pulse of Meridian:** An anonymous Sangromancer who secretly keeps an entire plague-ridden district alive by maintaining a blood-ward network from a basement laboratory. If they stop, hundreds die. They haven't slept in weeks.

### Associated Relics
- **The Arterial Codex:** A living book bound in preserved veins that records the blood-magic of its previous owners. New entries appear when the book is fed a drop of the reader's blood—but it also reveals the reader's physical secrets to anyone who reads it afterward.
- **Janos Audron's Heartbeat:** Not the physical heart, but a preserved echo—a crystal vial that pulses with the rhythm of the Last Ancient's stolen organ. Sangromancers who hold it can amplify blood-magic, but the vial whispers of imprisonment and loss.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A blood-well awakens; the circle that tended it has vanished.
	- A plague is actually a binding ritual miscast; cure requires a willing sacrifice.
	- A Hylden reliquary offers power for a city's heartbeat—literally.
	- The Pulse of Meridian has sent a desperate message: "The ward is failing. Bring B-negative. Bring hope." Time is running out.
	- A Sangromancer's Arterial Codex has been stolen. Whoever reads it now knows the blood-secrets of a dozen powerful figures. A bidding war—or a killing spree—is imminent.
- NPC mentor template: Name (arterial title) • Role (chirurgeon-magus) • Quirk (counts heartbeats aloud) • Ask/Price (sample or vow) • Teaches (taboo ethics, safe rites) • Hooks (runaway apprentice, vengeful patient).

## Glyphwright

Not a sorcerer, but a scavenger of divine intellect. The Glyphwright is an obsessive scholar who excavates the ruins of the Hylden, seeking to understand the alien physics of their technology. They see magic not as an art but as a set of blueprints left behind by a forgotten, superior race. By meticulously re-engineering Hylden artifacts, they create "structured magic"—devices of brass, crystal, and bone that replicate the miracles of their creators. But this knowledge comes at a terrible price. Prolonged exposure to these impossible geometries and non-Euclidean constructs inevitably frays the seeker's mind, leading to prophetic madness, physical mutations, and the constant whispering echo of the void in their thoughts.

Common threads:
- Engineers of reliable, structured magic (in contrast to blood sorcery).
- Often hunted by Sarafan inquisitors who brand them heretics.
- Carry heavy tomes or etched plates; their magic is prepared, not innate.

> **Distinguishing the Glyphwright from the Hylden Warlock**
> Both classes draw on Hylden-derived power, but their relationship to that power is fundamentally opposed. A **Glyphwright** is an *engineer and scholar*—they do not channel Hylden energy but *reverse-engineer* ancient alien technology, treating glyphs as blueprints to be decoded, replicated, and improved. Their risk is madness from exposure to incomprehensible physics: geometries that shouldn't fit in a mortal mind, equations that rewrite perception. They decipher the logic of alien gods, binding it into circuits of brass and bone at the cost of their sanity. A **Hylden Warlock** is a *conduit and apostate*—they have formed direct pacts with Hylden entities or the entropic void itself, channeling raw, chaotic power that is deeply and inherently corrupting. The Glyphwright's workbench is covered in chalk diagrams; the Warlock's altar is stained with void-residue. The Seeker fears losing their mind; the Warlock fears losing their soul.

### Roleplay Guidance

Glyphwrights are the closest thing Nosgoth has to scientists—and they act like it, for better and worse. They are methodical, curious, and deeply uncomfortable with uncertainty. Where a Sangromancer feels magic in their pulse, a Glyphwright writes it as an equation. They believe every problem is a geometry waiting to be solved, and every disaster is a formula that was written wrong.

- **Voice and manner:** Precise, pedantic. Tends to diagram conversations. Corrects others' pronunciation of glyph-terms with visible discomfort at the errors. Carries chalk or a stylus at all times.
- **Social habits:** Happiest in workshops. Bonds over shared puzzles. Deeply uncomfortable with emotional displays—will attempt to solve grief as if it were an engineering problem. Means well; lands poorly.
- **Fears:** Loss of mental clarity. A Glyphwright's mind is their instrument—corruption, madness, or the creeping influence of Hylden glyph-whispers is an existential threat, not just a personal one.

### Factional Ties
- **Pale Accord:** Natural allies. The Accord's research agenda depends on Glyphwrights, and the Accord protects them from Sarafan persecution—usually.
- **Sarafan Order:** Branded heretics. The Sarafan see Hylden glyph-magic as enemy technology, regardless of who wields it. Glyphwrights who fall to the Sarafan face the Lex Luminis tribunal.
- **Hylden Remnants:** A Glyphwright's relationship with the Hylden is like a scholar's relationship with a plague: fascinating, essential to study, and deadly if you get too close.
- **Outlands Guilds:** The Ashwalkers guild employs Glyphwrights as ruin-surveyors and trap-disablers. The pay is decent; the hazard insurance is nonexistent.

### Rites and Traditions
- **The Inscription Exam:** No dramatic initiation—just a test. The aspirant must inscribe a functioning ward from memory, under time pressure, with deliberate errors introduced into the template. If the ward works, they pass. If it explodes, they have other career options (assuming they survive).
- **The Chalk Circle:** Before any major glyph-working, Glyphwrights draw a containment circle in chalk and speak their intent aloud. It's partly practical (containment is important) and partly superstitious (the chalk "listens" and the glyph respects stated boundaries). No one wants to test whether the superstition is wrong.
- **Archival Oath:** All Glyphwrights swear to document their work. Failure to record a discovery is considered worse than failure to make one. "Knowledge unwritten is knowledge twice lost."

### Sacred Sites
- **The Lattice Vault:** A mountain vault where the oldest known Hylden glyph-plates are stored, maintained by a secretive academy of human and vampire seekers who have worked side-by-side for centuries. Access requires solving an entry-puzzle that changes weekly.
- **The Resonance Pillar:** A crystalline column discovered in the Northern wastes, near the Hylden Gate, that appears to amplify glyph-magic within its radius. Glyphwrights debate whether it's a tool, a beacon, or a trap. All three camps present compelling arguments.

### Famous Glyphwrights
- **Arcturus Sigil-Tongue:** A human Glyphwright who can *speak* glyphs—his voice alone activates certain ward-patterns, without inscription. He is the only known practitioner of this ability, and the Sarafan, the Accord, and the Hylden Remnants all want to know how.
- **Sev the Unglyphed:** A Glyphwright who was once corrupted by a Hylden device and had every glyph burned from her mind. She rebuilt her knowledge from scratch—different, stranger, and arguably more effective than before.

### Associated Relics
- **The Architect's Compass:** A Hylden-era drafting tool that draws perfect glyph-circles on any surface—including air. Wards drawn with this compass last twice as long.
- **The Lattice Primer:** The oldest known textbook of glyph-magic, written by an unknown author in a language that shifts based on the reader's level of understanding. Beginners see simple diagrams; masters see mathematical proofs that cause nosebleeds.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A city ward collapses every dusk; trace the missing sigil in the catacombs.
	- A moving caravan is actually a distributed glyph; steal one wagon and the spell unravels.
	- An Archglyph sentinel has gone feral; rebind or retire it without destroying the archive.
	- Arcturus Sigil-Tongue has been kidnapped. Three factions claim responsibility. At least one is lying. All three want his voice.
	- The Resonance Pillar near the Hylden Gate has started *transmitting*. Whatever it's saying, the glyph-seekers who decoded the first line all went quiet at the same time.
- NPC mentor template: Name (scribe-mark) • Role (ward architect) • Quirk (speaks in geometries) • Ask/Price (recover a broken rune) • Teaches (counter-glyphs, safe dismantling) • Hooks (political sabotage, forbidden lattice).

## Dreadblade

Assassins shaped into weapons, Dreadblades descend from Sarafan enforcers or cursed Zephonim war-survivors. They blend magic with martial precision, favoring single-strike kills and spectral poisons. Often solitary or deployed as elite scouts into cursed territories, their footfalls are auguries: every blade an execution, every step a death foretold.

Common threads:
- Lineages in inquisitions, black ops, and war remnants.
- Executioner’s pragmatism—strike once, vanish utterly.
- Unsettling mix of discipline, fatalism, and ritual purity.
> **Distinguishing the Dreadblade from the Shadowmancer**
> Both classes share Zephonim roots, but they are fundamentally different responses to the same lineage. A **Shadowmancer** is shaped by Zephonim *culture*—the web-spire philosophy, the belief that shadows are a weaveable substance, the patient art of seeing without being seen. They are arcane manipulators who *embody* the spider-clan's worldview. A **Dreadblade** is shaped by *war and trauma*—the Sarafan inquisitions, battlefield curses, the brutal pragmatism of survival. A Zephonim who becomes a Dreadblade has *rejected* the contemplative spire-life in favor of the executioner's clarity; they do not weave shadows but wield steel through them. The Shadowmancer asks "What can I learn from the dark?" The Dreadblade asks "What can I kill in it?"
### Roleplay Guidance

Dreadblades are not murderers. They are *finishers*—the final punctuation in a sentence someone else began. They take no pleasure in killing (the best ones don't), but they take profound pride in doing it *well*. A clean kill is an art form. A messy one is a confession of incompetence.

- **Voice and manner:** Economy of words. Says in three words what others say in twenty. Sits with their back to a wall and their dominant hand free. When they smile, it's either genuine warmth or the last thing someone sees—context matters.
- **Social habits:** Surprisingly good listeners—a professional requirement. May cook for the party, clean their blades with meditative care, or maintain equipment with ritualistic precision. Relaxation looks like controlled stillness.
- **Fears:** Losing control. A Dreadblade's worst fear is killing someone they didn't intend to—a stray strike, a poisoned blade in the wrong hands, a contract based on lies.

### Factional Ties
- **Sarafan Order:** Historical origin point. Some Dreadblades were once Sarafan executioners or "Veil Operatives." The Order still maintains a secretive Dreadblade-adjacent unit called the Silent Chapter, though officially it doesn't exist.
- **Zephonim Clan:** The other ancestral line. Zephonim war-survivors developed Dreadblade techniques independently, focused on spectral venoms and vertical assassination. Zephonim Dreadblades are called "Fang-Descenders."
- **Outlands Guilds:** The Red Ledger bounty guild is the primary employer of freelance Dreadblades. Contracts are sealed with a blood-drop on parchment—binding and traceable.
- **Vampire Clans:** Clan matriarchs employ Dreadblades as "whisper-envoys"—messengers whose very presence is a threat, carrying words that don't need to be spoken aloud.

### Rites and Traditions
- **The Naming of the Blade:** A Dreadblade names their primary weapon after their first completed contract. The name is spoken once, to the blade, in private. It is never shared. If the blade breaks, a Dreadblade enters a period of mourning identical to the death of a partner.
- **The Last Word:** Before executing a contract, a Dreadblade offers the target a chance to speak—one sentence, uninterrupted. The Dreadblade memorizes it. Veteran Dreadblades carry decades of last words in their memory like a private library of endings.
- **The Clean Walk:** After a completed contract, a Dreadblade walks in a straight line for exactly one mile before stopping, speaking, or looking back. It is a purification rite—what lies behind is finished.

### Sacred Sites
- **The House of Last Words:** A hidden crypt where veteran Dreadblades inscribe the last words of their contracts on the walls. Centuries of final sentences cover the stone. Reading them is overwhelming—a chorus of the almost-dead, desperate and mundane in equal measure.
- **The Silent Garden:** A walled courtyard at the summit of a Zephonim spire, where no sound can exist. New Dreadblades complete their final trial in absolute silence—fighting, killing, and surviving without the comfort of sound.

### Famous Dreadblades
- **"Quietus":** A Dreadblade of legendary efficiency who completed forty-seven contracts over thirty years before retiring to operate a teahouse in a human settlement. The tea is excellent. The teahouse has never been robbed. No one talks about why.
- **Descent-of-Silk:** A Zephonim Fang-Descender who assassinates exclusively from above, dropping from ceilings and canopies. She has never been seen at ground level by her targets. Her calling card is a single strand of spider-silk left at the scene.

### Associated Relics
- **The Edge of Mercy:** A blade that kills painlessly—victims don't realize they've been struck until the weapon is withdrawn. Coveted by Dreadblades who consider clean kills a matter of professional ethics.
- **Venom of the Last Breath:** A spectral poison distilled from Zephonim venom glands. A single drop causes a painless paralysis that allows the target one final sentence before the end. Dreadblades consider it the most respectful method.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A contract targets a "monster" who is actually a key witness; swap death for disappearance.
	- Spectral venom supplies are tainted; find the source before blades turn on their wielders.
	- A cult trades names for protection; sever the ledger without starting a war.
	- "Quietus" has received a contract they refuse to execute. Someone wants them dead for that refusal. The party must protect an assassin—or discover why the contract was issued.
	- The House of Last Words has been broken into. Someone is using the inscriptions to resurrect completed contracts—not the dead, but the *jobs*.
- NPC mentor template: Name (silent title) • Role (handler or knife-monk) • Quirk (collects last words) • Ask/Price (one truth as collateral) • Teaches (extraction, clean exits) • Hooks (burned cover, moral line).

## Warden of Balance

A Warden of Balance is a soul sworn to a thankless, impossible duty: tending the spiritual wounds of the Pillars. They are not priests of a dead religion but caretakers of a dying world. Through deep meditation at the sites of the Pillars, they receive fractured, agonizing visions of the past and future, using this fragmented insight to act as part priest, part enforcer, and part oracle. They wield authority granted by no king, seeking to prune timelines that lead to absolute ruin and destroy those whose unchecked power—be it vampiric, human, or Hylden—threatens to unmake Nosgoth entirely. Theirs is a lonely path of duty over power, sacrifice over glory.

Common threads:
- Oaths to weigh ends against means.
- Pilgrimages between sites where the veils fray.
- Ally and adversary alike test their definitions of “balance.”

### Roleplay Guidance

Wardens of Balance carry the heaviest burden in Nosgoth: the obligation to be fair in a world that isn't. They do not seek victory—they seek *equilibrium*, which often means ensuring that no one wins too much. This makes them frustrating allies and terrifying arbiters. A Warden doesn't take sides; they take *measure*.

- **Voice and manner:** Formal, weighted. Speaks as if each word is being recorded for eternity (in their minds, it is). Pauses before answering—not for thought, but to check whether the answer serves balance or merely serves someone.
- **Social habits:** Makes lists of debts—who owes what to whom, across all factions. Maintains a personal Judgment Wheel (a written record of moral calculations). Will interrupt a party celebration to note that a favor is still unpaid.
- **Fears:** Making the wrong call. Not the consequences—the *wrongness*. A Warden who misjudges balance doesn't just fail; they become part of the corruption. Every error is existential.

### Factional Ties
- **The Shattered Pillars:** Wardens are sworn to the *concept* of the Pillars, not the current ruins. Some Wardens believe restoration is possible; others believe the Pillars must be reinvented entirely. This theological split defines Warden politics.
- **Sarafan Order:** Complex. Some Wardens were once Sarafan who grew disillusioned with zealotry. The Order considers Wardens "soft heretics"—not evil, just dangerously naive about the nature of balance.
- **Vampire Clans:** Wardens adjudicate disputes between clans in exchange for neutrality. A Warden's ruling carries weight precisely because they have no stake in clan politics. Disrespecting a Warden's judgment is rare—and costly.
- **Cult of the Wheel:** The Wardens' oldest enemy. The Cult preaches balance through surrender to fate; the Wardens preach balance through active maintenance. Same word, incompatible definitions.

### Rites and Traditions
- **The Investiture of Scales:** A Warden's initiation requires them to judge a real dispute—a case brought before them with genuine stakes and genuine anger. They must render a verdict that satisfies neither party completely but prevents violence. If both sides leave unhappy but alive, the Warden has passed.
- **The Pillar Walk:** An annual pilgrimage to the ruins of the Pillars, during which Wardens meditate at each pillar-site and assess the state of its corresponding principle. The walk takes weeks. Some Wardens return changed—quieter, older in the eyes, carrying truths they won't share.
- **The Weighing of Oaths:** Before making any binding promise, a Warden places two stones on a physical scale—one for consequence, one for intent. If the scale balances, the oath is sworn. If it tips, the Warden must reconsider. The stones are ordinary; the exercise is the point.

### Sacred Sites
- **The Pillar Ruins:** The primary sacred site. Even shattered, the Pillars pulse with residual energy. Wardens who meditate there sometimes receive visions—of what balance looked like, what corruption feels like, and what restoration might require.
- **The Hall of Equilibrium:** A hidden chamber beneath a forgotten temple where the first Wardens carved their founding oaths into the walls alongside counterarguments to their own principles. The Hall teaches that balance begins with doubting yourself. *(Cross-reference: World Primer — Key Locations for the site's geographical and narrative context.)*

### Famous Wardens
- **Arbiter Caelus:** The last Warden who attempted to restore a Pillar (the Pillar of Conflict). He succeeded in stabilizing it for three days before the effort killed him. His method—a combination of blood-magic, glyph-work, and sheer willpower—is studied by every Warden since, though none have replicated it.
- **The Blind Judge:** A Warden who put out her own eyes to eliminate bias from visual perception. She judges by voice, heartbeat, and the resonance of oaths. Her rulings are considered the most just in living memory. She is also the most difficult person in Nosgoth to lie to.

### Associated Relics
- **The Scales of Ariel:** A set of golden scales said to have belonged to the original Guardian of Balance. They do not weigh objects—they weigh intentions. Place a promise on one side and its shadow on the other, and the scales reveal whether the promise is sincere.
- **The Judgment Wheel:** A rotating mechanism inscribed with the nine Pillar principles. Wardens use it to map moral complexities, spinning the wheel to see which principle a dilemma most threatens.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A Judgment Wheel law is abused to oppress a district; rewrite it in ceremony.
	- Two miracles claim the Pillars' blessing—only one is real, and both have followers.
	- A time-stasis reliquary fails, releasing a century-old grievance.
	- The Blind Judge has rendered a verdict that angers every faction simultaneously. Someone will try to kill her before dawn. The party must decide whether to protect her judgment—or overturn it.
	- A Warden's Pillar Walk has revealed something unprecedented: one of the Pillars is *regenerating*. Not much. Not fast. But enough to start a war over who controls the healing process.
- NPC mentor template: Name (title of office) • Role (arbiter-priest) • Quirk (never turns their back on anyone) • Ask/Price (mediate a feud) • Teaches (oathcraft, lawful rites) • Hooks (schismatic Warden, corrupted edict).

## Hylden Warlock

Exiles, traitors, and entropy-scarred prophets, Hylden Warlocks draw on glyph decay, soul leeching, and forbidden prophecy. Many fled the Spectral Realm bearing fragments of the Wheel itself. They twist time’s threads and sanity’s seams. To them, power is proof—god and gospel in one.

Common threads:
- Apostate scholars with void-marked minds.
- Entropic rites, grim bargains, and weaponized despair.
- Pursued by Wardens; courted by factions that prefer ends to means.

### Roleplay Guidance

Hylden Warlocks are the loneliest characters in Nosgoth—not because they lack company, but because their perspective is fundamentally alien. They have seen behind the curtain, touched the machinery of fate, and concluded that the universe is a system that can be hacked. This makes them brilliant, dangerous, and profoundly unsettling to be around.

- **Voice and manner:** Speaks in tangents that turn out to be the point. References events that haven't happened yet, or happened to someone else, or happened in a timeline that no longer exists. Eyes have a tendency to focus on things that aren't there.
- **Social habits:** Catalogues everything. Takes notes in margins of other people's books. Forms intense, brief attachments—then withdraws when the "equation resolves." Genuinely baffled by small talk.
- **Fears:** The Wheel catching up. A Hylden Warlock knows they are defying the cosmic order, and the cosmic order has a long memory. Every Warlock carries the quiet certainty that one day, the universe will notice them—and correct its mistake.

### Factional Ties
- **Hylden Remnants:** The ancestral connection. Some Warlocks serve the Remnants willingly; others were created by them and escaped. The relationship ranges from loyalty to loathing, often in the same individual.
- **Wardens of Balance:** Natural adversaries. Wardens see Hylden Warlocks as walking corruption—entropy made flesh. Warlocks see Wardens as the universe's hall monitors.
- **Pale Accord:** The most willing employer. The Accord's Chapter of Time and Chapter of Dimension both use Warlock consultants, paying in knowledge and looking the other way about methodology.
- **Crimson Fracture:** Some Warlocks find common ground with the Fracture's merging agenda—after all, the Hylden's original goal was to break boundaries. Others see the Fracture as amateurs who don't understand what they're breaking.
- **Cult of Hash'ak'gik:** The Hylden Remnants recognize Hash'ak'gik as an unwelcome interloper—not Hylden, not Elder God, but something that competes for the same reality-bending frequencies the Warlocks exploit. Smart Warlocks treat the Cult as a threat older than their benefactors and twice as unpredictable; the demon has survived both the Ancient War and Kain's empire without taking sides, which makes it dangerous in ways raw power cannot account for.

### Rites and Traditions
- **The Void Baptism:** Initiation requires the aspirant to open a controlled breach into the Hylden dimension, endure the whispers for one full minute, and close the breach without assistance. Most aspirants last thirty seconds. The ones who emerge sane-enough are Warlocks. The ones who emerge *too sane* are suspected of being Hylden plants.
- **The Entropy Offering:** Before any major working, a Warlock destroys something meaningful to themselves—a memento, a relationship, a memory. The logic is that entropy must be fed, and feeding it intentionally is better than having it taken. Some Warlocks keep a box of sacrificial objects; others sacrifice the box.
- **The Calculation of Regret:** A Warlock keeps a private ledger of every cost their magic has extracted—from themselves and from others. On the anniversary of their Void Baptism, they read the ledger aloud to themselves. It is the only tradition every Warlock observes, and the only one they never discuss.

### Sacred Sites
- **The Whispering Gate:** A sealed Hylden portal in the Northern mountains, where the barrier between dimensions is thinnest. Warlocks meditate near it to hear fragments of Hylden communication—equations, arguments, pleas, and threats, all tangled together. Extended exposure causes nosebleeds, timeline confusion, and the unsettling sensation that your reflection is moving independently.
- **The Void Library:** A conceptual space that may not physically exist—a collection of knowledge that Warlocks access through glyph-meditation. Books appear and disappear. Some contain information from the future. Some contain information from timelines that never occurred. All are accurate, and none are trustworthy.

### Famous Hylden Warlocks
- **Axiom the Regretful:** A Warlock who calculated the exact cost of closing the Hylden Gate permanently—and refused to share the number. He claims the price is payable but "no one would be willing." He's been trying to find an alternative for twenty years.
- **The Equation:** Not a person but a phenomenon—a wandering Hylden spell that has achieved something resembling sentience. It appears as numbers scratched into surfaces, and anyone who reads the sequence gains temporary access to Hylden glyph-knowledge at the cost of a memory they can never recover.

### Associated Relics
- **The Shard of the Unwheel:** A fragment of the Wheel of Fate, broken loose during Raziel's sacrifice. It negates fate-based effects in its vicinity—prophecies fail, divination goes blank, and destiny stutters. Carrying it makes you invisible to the Elder God. It also makes you invisible to luck.
- **The Void Stylus:** A writing instrument that inscribes in entropy—whatever it writes on decays. Used to create temporary glyphs that dissolve after activation, leaving no evidence. The stylus itself is slowly consuming the hand that holds it.

### Using this in play
- Quest seeds:
	- A Decay Field is eating a neighborhood's memories; map and anchor them before they fade.
	- A doom-prophecy spreads like a plague of words; break its cadence or break its author.
	- A banished entity answers questions—for a price measured in sanity.
	- Axiom the Regretful has gone silent. His last message was a number: 9. What does it mean? The Pale Accord, the Wardens, and the Hylden Remnants are all looking for him.
	- The Equation has appeared in a population center. People are reading it. People are forgetting things. The sequence is spreading like a virus, and whatever it's building toward, it's nearly complete.
- NPC mentor template: Name (void sobriquet) • Role (exile-prophet) • Quirk (blinks out mid-sentence) • Ask/Price (carry a shard, accept a scar) • Teaches (safe channels, lines not to cross) • Hooks (Hylden conclave, price of knowledge).

## Inter-Class Tensions

Nosgoth's classes are not just mechanical choices—they represent philosophical commitments that inevitably collide. GMs and players can use these tensions to fuel party dynamics, NPC rivalries, and faction conflicts.

| Class Pair | Nature of Tension | Roleplaying Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Blood Knight vs. Dreadblade** | Open combat vs. assassination. Blood Knights consider assassination dishonorable; Dreadblades consider frontal assault wasteful. | Each secretly envies the other's certainty. |
| **Soul Reaver vs. Hylden Warlock** | Both walk between worlds, but Soul Reavers seek harmony with the realms while Warlocks seek to exploit them. | Fascination masked as contempt—they understand each other too well. |
| **Shadowmancer vs. Warden of Balance** | Secrecy vs. transparency. Wardens demand accountability; Shadowmancers consider it a death sentence. | A Warden who works with a Shadowmancer has already compromised; a Shadowmancer who trusts a Warden has already lost. |
| **Sangromancer vs. Glyphwright** | Organic magic vs. structured magic. Blood sorcery is intuitive, messy, and alive; glyph-magic is precise, clean, and cold. | Mutual professional disdain. Each considers the other's methodology primitive. |
| **Warden of Balance vs. Hylden Warlock** | Order vs. entropy. The Wardens' oldest theological enemy. | Genuine moral conflict—Wardens *need* to believe Warlocks can be redeemed; Warlocks need to believe Wardens are wrong. |
| **Blood Knight vs. Sangromancer** | Both use blood, but Blood Knights use it as fuel while Sangromancers use it as medium. Blood Knights see Sangromancers as parasites wearing a healer's mask. | Can become a powerful partnership if trust is established—the Knight protects, the Sangromancer sustains. |
